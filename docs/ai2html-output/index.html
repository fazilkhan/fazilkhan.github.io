No Country for Dissent

Indian Govt is using colonial-era sedition law to put its critics, activists, and protestors behind bars

By Fazil Khan

Published June 28, 2021

In late 2019, during the anti-Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA), which discriminated against Muslims, protests, at least 25 sedition cases were filed by the police. Similarly, last year in September, at least 22 cases were filed with charges of sedition during the outrage against a brutal gangrape in the state of Uttar Pradesh.

Since the BJP came into power in May 2014, at least 516 sedition cases have been filed across the country. More than half of these cases were filed for “derogatory” remarks against the Prime Minister Modi and the UP chief minister Yogi Adityanath. In comparison, 279 cases of sedition were filed during the UPA regime between 2010 and May, 2014.

Riding on a wave of Hindu Nationalism, Narendra Modi became the prime minister of India in 2014 with an absolute majority in the lower house of the parliament – a feat achieved after three decades by any single party. Over the past six years, his party has been accused of fueling communal tensions, curbing freedom of speech and encouraging militant hindu nationalist activities in the country. In 2019, Modi returned to power with an improved mandate.

Of almost 11,000 people booked under charges related to sedition, two-thirds were charged after BJP came to power in 2014.
